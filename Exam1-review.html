    Section 2.1 Review for Exam 1

    Exam 1 covers the following sections from the book:
    🔗
    • Chapter 1: 1.5
      🔗
    • Chapter 2: 2.1-2.12
      🔗
    • Chapter 3: 3.1-3.5 (selected topics, as discussed in class)
      🔗
    • Chapter 4: 4.1-4.4 (selected topics, as discussed in class)
      🔗
    • Chapter 5: 5.1 (for 5.1 only up to and including page 134)
      🔗
    Here are some skills that are needed towards problem solving. You need to be able to:
    🔗
    1. Work with permutations, use cycle notation, compute signature of a permutation, find the matrix associated to a permutation.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Give the definition of a: group/subgroup, homomorphism, and use it to prove various properties.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Prove that a given set is a subgroup (closure, identity, inverses)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Use the notions of lcm and gcd of two integers and their properties.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Prove that a given set is a normal subgroup (using either the definition, or cosets, or writing it as a kernel). Also, be able to explain why a given subgroup is not be normal.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Prove that a given group or subgroup is cyclic or not cyclic.
      🔗
      🔗
    7. Prove that a given function is a homomorphism. Compute the kernel and the image. Decide whether the homomorphism is injective or surjective.
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Prove that two given groups are isomorphic by constructing an isomorphism (or other arguments, such as: they are both cyclic and have the same number of elements).
      🔗
      🔗
    9. Use the Product Proposition to prove isomorphisms of groups
      🔗
      🔗
    10. Argue (most likely by contradiction) why two given groups are not isomorphic. (e.g. explain that one is abelian and the other one is not, or one is cyclic and the other one is not)
      🔗
      🔗
    11. Work with cosets and the concept of quotient group. In particular, use the fact that cosets form a partition, and understand when two cosets are equal to each other.
      🔗
      🔗
    12. Apply the First Isomorphism Theorem to prove isomorphisms and to compute orders of groups, or the index of a subgroup.
      🔗
      🔗
    13. Use the Lagrange theorem and its consequences in a variety of problems
      🔗
      🔗
    14. Work with the notion of order of an element. Find orders of elements, prove results about orders.
      🔗
      🔗
    15. Prove that a given relation is an equivalence relation
      🔗
      🔗
    16. Prove that a given function is well-defined.
      🔗
      🔗
    17. Work with matrices, determinants, integers modulo \(n\text{,}\) integers, rational, real and complex numbers.
      🔗
      🔗
    18. Work with fields, and in particular \(\mathbb F_p\text{;}\) Know how to compute inverses of elements in \(\mathbb F_p\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    19. Use basic notions of linear algebra: vector space, subspace, basis, linear transformation. Prove a set is a subspace, find a basis, prove that a function is a linear transformation, prove that 2 vector spaces are isomorphic.
      🔗
      🔗
    20. Find the matrix of linear transformation with respect to a choice of bases.
      🔗
      🔗
    21. Reason using the concepts of orthogonal matrix and orthogonal operator.
      🔗
      🔗
